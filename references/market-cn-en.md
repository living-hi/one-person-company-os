# Market Strategy: Chinese And English Priority Markets

## Purpose

Use this reference when the one-person company should operate globally while treating Chinese-speaking and English-speaking markets as the two priority execution surfaces.

Do not frame this as "one global market with translation." Treat it as one company with:
- a shared operating core
- a shared product truth
- two important language and channel environments

## Positioning Rule

Default positioning:
- global product
- bilingual operating system
- Chinese and English as first-class markets

Good framing:
- one company, two core language markets
- shared product, localized go-to-market
- global operating model with market-specific execution

Weak framing:
- China-only unless the user explicitly wants that
- English-only unless the user explicitly wants that
- "just translate everything"

## What Should Stay Shared

Keep these shared unless there is a strong reason to diverge:
- mission
- company charter
- product core
- product roadmap logic
- pricing philosophy
- quality bar
- operating rhythm
- role system

## What Should Usually Be Localized

Expect to localize:
- positioning language
- landing page copy
- distribution channels
- content calendar
- sales scripts
- onboarding examples
- support FAQs
- compliance assumptions
- payment and platform expectations

## Default Market Split

When the user has not chosen otherwise, use:
- primary market: whichever market best matches the current traction source
- secondary market: the other priority language market

If no traction exists yet:
- choose one market for validation
- keep the second market in the plan, not in the initial scope

Do not force simultaneous full launches unless the user clearly has the bandwidth.

## Product Strategy Rule

Prefer:
- one product core
- one codebase if feasible
- one operating model
- market-specific copy, channels, and support layers

Avoid:
- two separate products too early
- duplicated roadmaps without evidence
- premature geo-specific complexity in the product itself

## Go-To-Market Guidance

For each market, define separately:
- ICP
- pain framing
- trust signals
- acquisition channels
- objection patterns
- onboarding friction
- support expectations

Then define what remains shared across both.

## Messaging Guidance

Do not directly translate slogans and assume they work.

Instead, adapt:
- emotional framing
- proof style
- credibility cues
- level of directness
- channel-native formatting

The output should preserve the same core promise while sounding native to the target market.

## Recommended Output Pattern For Bilingual Work

When the user asks for market strategy or launch assets, structure the output as:
1. shared company core
2. Chinese market strategy
3. English market strategy
4. shared artifacts
5. localized artifacts
6. sequencing recommendation

## Sequencing Rule

If resources are constrained, use this order:
1. validate in the market with the shortest path to signal
2. stabilize product and message
3. localize for the second priority market
4. unify learnings into the shared operating system

## Risks To Flag

Surface these risks early:
- assuming channel behavior is the same across markets
- underestimating localization effort
- mixing compliance assumptions
- copying support language across markets without adaptation
- trying to launch fully in both markets before one has signal
